# Annapurna Base Camp Trek during Monsoon
by [Paul Gurung](https://www.discoveryworldtrekking.com/blog/author/Paul)
ON 31 Oct, 2025
![Himalayan Image](https://cms.discoveryworldtrekking.com/media/6875/Annapurna-Base-Camp-Trek-during-Monsoon.webp)
The Annapurna Base Camp Trek during the Monsoon is often overlooked in favor of the more popular spring and autumn seasons. The monsoon season mostly starts just before mid-June and lasts until the last week of September. This period accounts for about 80% of the country's annual rainfall.
Nepal’s south-west monsoon, born on the Bay of Bengal, first drenches the eastern foothills before slowly cloaking the Annapurna region in vibrant moisture. Over its roughly 112-day span, every footfall echoes with the soft patter of rain, as tiny rivulets swell into roaring waterfalls and the sanctuary’s secret moods emerge.
For the adventurous soul or the budget-conscious traveler, monsoon trekking offers a unique and deeply rewarding experience. From lush, rain-fed landscapes to quieter trails and discounted rates, this off-season journey reveals a side of the Himalayas that few get to witness. While the rain brings its own set of challenges, it also breathes life into the region, transforming the Annapurna corridor into a vibrant, emerald sanctuary.
![](https://cms.discoveryworldtrekking.com/media/6877/Annapurna%20base%20camp.webp?w=1280&h=720)
## Why Annapurna Base Camp Trek during Monsoon?
Monsoon Annapurna Base Camp Trek (ABC) means choosing intimacy over crowds. Teahouses echo with the cadence of rain rather than the buzz of busy dining rooms. With spring and autumn trekkers holding sway in March–May and September–November, June–August trails fall into a quiet rhythm that invites contemplation. Fewer fellow hikers mean early-morning solitude at Machapuchare Viewpoint and lingering cups of masala chai in Chhomrong as mist coils through the pines.
Beyond serenity, monsoon trekkers reap tangible savings. Teahouse rates dip by as much as 20–30%. Budget travelers can extend their stays without stretching costs—ideal for lingering two extra nights in Bamboo or Deurali to catch that elusive clear morning skyline.
Monsoon trekking yields a sensory tapestry that the spring season can’t match. The earth’s fragrance after rain, the deep green of wet rhododendron forests, and the thunderous roar of swollen cascades. Every vista feels alive and in flux, a privilege reserved for those willing to embrace rain-kissed trails.
With fewer trekkers around, interactions with locals become more personal and meaningful. You’ll have more opportunities to learn about Gurung and Magar traditions, enjoy home-cooked meals, and perhaps even join in local festivals that occur during the rainy months.
![](https://cms.discoveryworldtrekking.com/media/6878/weather%20conditions.webp?w=1280&h=720)
## Weather Conditions
Nepal receives approximately 80% of its annual rainfall during the monsoon season. The eastern and central regions, including the Annapurna trail corridor, experience the heaviest precipitation. For instance:
  * Pokhara, the gateway to ABC, receives up to 4,000 mm of rain annually, with July being the wettest month.
  * Ghandruk and Chhomrong, key villages along the trail, also receive substantial rainfall, often exceeding 3,000 mm.


Rain typically falls in the late afternoon or evening, leaving mornings relatively clear. This pattern allows trekkers to start early and reach their destination before the downpour begins.
### Temperature and Visibility
Daytime temperatures at lower elevations range from 20°C to 25°C, while higher altitudes remain cooler. Nights can be chilly but not freezing. Visibility varies—cloud cover is common, but early mornings often offer stunning, fleeting views of snow-capped peaks like Machapuchare and Annapurna I before the clouds roll in.
![](https://cms.discoveryworldtrekking.com/media/6879/monsoon%20trekking%20gear.webp?w=1280&h=720)
## Essential Gear for Monsoon Trekking
Trekking during the monsoon demands thoughtful packing. Staying dry and comfortable is key to enjoying the journey. Here’s what you’ll need:
  * Waterproof Backpack Cover: Keeps your gear dry during sudden downpours.
  * Breathable Rain Jacket and Pants: Look for lightweight, ventilated options to avoid overheating.
  * Quick-Dry Clothing: Synthetic or merino wool layers are ideal; avoid cotton as it retains moisture.
  * Gaiters: Protect your legs from mud and leeches, especially in forested areas.
  * Trekking Poles: Provide stability on slippery, uneven terrain.
  * Waterproof Hiking Boots: Ensure they have a good grip and are broken in before the trek.
  * Dry Bags or Ziplock Packs: Essential for protecting electronics, documents, and clothing.
  * Insect Repellent: Leeches and mosquitoes are more active during this season.
  * Headlamp with Extra Batteries: Power outages are common in remote areas.
  * Microfiber Towel and Extra Socks: Quick-drying and compact, these are lifesavers in wet conditions.
  * Optional but helpful items include an umbrella for light rain, anti-leech socks, and a lightweight poncho that can cover both you and your backpack.

We also expect you to carry a small backpack for things you will need frequently, valuables, and important documents.
## Safety Tips
While monsoon trekking is rewarding, it’s not without risks. Here are key safety tips to ensure a smooth and secure journey:
**Start Early**
Setting out by 6–7 AM isn’t just about beating the crowds—it’s a tactical move to stay a step ahead of the monsoon’s daily rhythm. In the morning, skies tend to be clearer, trails firmer, and river levels lower, giving you safer footing and better visibility on steep ascents or tricky descents.
By reaching your day’s destination before the clouds roll in, you minimize exposure to sudden downpours, reduce the risk of landslides on saturated slopes, and unlock those fleeting, breathtaking mountain vistas that often vanish by midday.
**Monitor Weather Forecasts**
In the mountains, the weather can change in minutes, so staying informed is very important. Before each day’s trek, check multiple sources—reliable trekking apps, local radio bulletins in teahouses, and your guide’s firsthand reports. You must pay special attention to warnings about flash-flood-prone areas and known landslide zones, especially in narrow valleys where a heavy shower can trigger debris flows.
Consistent updates help you choose the safest routes, adjust your schedule, and even postpone a segment of your trek if conditions turn hazardous. If you trek with us, our guides will ensure your safety in adverse conditions as well.
**Avoid River Crossings During Rain**
In the mountains, what feels like a shallow ford in fair weather can become a treacherous torrent in minutes. During or immediately after a downpour, water levels rise quickly, currents strengthen, and riverbanks erode, making crossings both unpredictable and perilous.
Whenever possible, you must cross major streams in the early morning, scout for sturdy suspension bridges downstream, and never attempt a ford if the water reaches your knees. When in doubt, it is always wise to wait out the rain or reroute via a safer path.
  
**Hire a Local Guide**
Local guides aren’t just navigators; they’re your safety net in the monsoon’s capricious domain. Their intimate knowledge of microclimates, alternate alignments around washouts, and timing of valley-specific showers can turn an ordeal into a memorable adventure.
Beyond hazard avoidance, guides enrich your journey with cultural insights—introducing you to hidden shrines, explaining traditional rain-season rituals, and negotiating the best tea-house rates. Investing in local expertise also supports the communities that steward these trails.
  
**Stay Hydrated and Nourished**
High humidity can mask your body’s thirst signals, making it easy to go hours without replenishing fluids, especially when you’re focused on navigating muddy paths. Yet dehydration slows your pace, impairs decision-making, and worsens altitude adjustment.
Remember to carry at least two liters of water in a bladder or reusable plastic bottle, plus water-purification tablets or a filter to refill from fresh streams. Pack calorie-dense snacks—nuts, energy bars, and trail mix—to sustain energy levels when appetite dips in the damp chill.
  
**Add Buffer Days**
Monsoon trekking demands a flexible itinerary. Land-slips can close trails without warning, local buses may halt services mid-season, and sudden storms can strand you in villages that fill up fast. By building in one or two extra days, you create breathing room to wait out heavy rains, reroute around damaged paths, or catch the next available transport.
This buffer reduces stress, keeps you from racing against the clock, and often leads to serendipitous discoveries—an unplanned waterfall, a hidden mountain festival, or a chance to bond longer with fellow trekkers.
  
**Travel Insurance**
Regular coverage isn’t enough when you’re chasing clouds in a landslide-prone region. Your policy must explicitly include emergency helicopter evacuation up to the highest trek altitude (Annapurna Base Camp: 4,130 m) and health care costs. 
Before you depart, scrutinize the fine print of your insurance: check altitude limits, pre-existing condition clauses, and exclusions for “adventure activities.” Having the right insurance not only protects your finances but also gives you peace of mind, so you can focus on the rhythm of the rain.
If you travel with us, our head office will be in contact with all trek teams at least once daily via the trek guide.
## Conclusion
The Annapurna Base Camp Trek during Monsoon is not for the faint of heart, but for those willing to embrace the rain, it offers a rare and intimate Himalayan experience. The trails are quieter, the landscapes more vibrant, and the cultural encounters more authentic. With proper preparation, the right gear, and a flexible mindset, monsoon trekking can be just as fulfilling—if not more so—than trekking in peak seasons.  
  
So if you're seeking an adventure that trades crowds for cascading waterfalls and misty mountain mornings, consider lacing up your boots this monsoon. The Annapurna region, drenched in green and alive with the rhythm of rain, awaits.
